Chapter 5: Visualizing High-Dimensional Biology (PCA and Clustering)

Published

June 5, 2026

Modified

July 5, 2026

Dimensionality Reduction Compresses Space, Not Signal

An RNA-seq dataset measuring 20,000 genes across 50 samples is not a simple spreadsheet; it is a mathematical matrix in 20,000-dimensional space. Every sample is a vector positioned along 20,000 gene coordinate axes. The human brain cannot visualize anything beyond three dimensions. We must rely on linear algebra to rotate and compress this high-dimensional space into a two-dimensional plot, allowing us to see how biological samples relate.

Core Concepts

In genomics, the data you analyze has far more dimensions (variables) than samples. This is the high-dimensional data problem.

However, genes do not act independently. Biological processes (like cell division, immune activation, or tissue differentiation) involve the coordinated expression of hundreds of genes simultaneously. This means the true biological signal does not occupy 20,000 independent dimensions—it is low-rank, meaning it is governed by a small number of coordinated biological pathways.

We use Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to find these coordinated signals and project them onto a 2D or 3D plot.

PCA: Intuitive Coordinate Rotation

Imagine a cloud of points representing your samples. PCA rotates this cloud of data points in high-dimensional space until it finds the direction of maximum variation.

  • Principal Component 1 (PC1): The single coordinate axis along which the data points are most spread out. It captures the strongest signal (variance) in the dataset.

  • Principal Component 2 (PC2): The second axis, chosen to be completely perpendicular (orthogonal) to PC1, capturing the next largest amount of variation.

By plotting your samples using PC1 as the X-axis and PC2 as the Y-axis, you compress 20,000 genes into a simple 2D scatter plot.

How to Read a PCA Plot

  1. PCA Scores (The Sample Points): Each point on a PCA plot represents a single biological sample. If two sample points are close to each other on the plot, it means they have highly similar transcriptomes. If they are far apart, their gene expression profiles differ.

  2. PCA Loadings (The Genes): Loadings are weights that define how much each gene contributes to a given PC. A gene with a high positive loading on PC1 is highly expressed in samples positioned on the right side of the plot and lowly expressed in samples on the left. Loadings connect the mathematical axes back to biological pathways.

  3. Variance Explained: Every PC has an associated percentage (e.g., “PC1: 45% variance explained”). This tells you what proportion of the total transcriptomic variation is captured by that PC. If PC1 and PC2 together explain 60% of the variance, a 2D scatter plot represents the majority of the signal in your data.

The Scree Plot: Deciding How Many PCs Matter

A scree plot shows the percentage of variance explained by each Principal Component in descending order.

You look for the “elbow” in the plot—the point where the variance explained drops sharply and flattens out. The PCs before the elbow contain the biological signal; the flat tail after the elbow contains random technical noise. For downstream analyses (like clustering), researchers typically keep only the top PCs (usually 10 to 30) before the elbow, discarding the noise-only dimensions.

Biological Interpretation

PCA is the primary quality control tool in genomics. Before you analyze your data for biological significance, you must audit the PCA plot.

Rule 1: Color by Batch Before You Color by Biology

When you receive your sequencing data, the first thing you should do is plot a PCA of the samples and color the points by technical variables (e.g., sequencing run day, library preparation batch, or technician).

  • If the samples cluster cleanly by biological group (e.g., tumor vs. normal), your biological signal is strong.

  • If the samples cluster by the date they were prepped, the primary driver of variation in your dataset is a technical batch effect. You must correct for this batch effect before you can trust any biological discoveries (as discussed in Chapter 6).

Rule 2: Inspect the Loadings for Biological Sanity

Do not treat the principal components as abstract numbers. Look at the top 10 genes with the highest loadings on PC1 and PC2.

  • In cancer samples, PC1 is often driven by cell proliferation genes (e.g., MKI67, PCNA).

  • If your top loadings are dominated by mitochondrial genes (e.g., MT-CO1), it is a technical warning sign: high mitochondrial expression indicates dying cells, meaning the primary variation in your experiment is cell death, not biology.

Summary and Required Reading

  1. Genomic data is high-dimensional: PCA compresses thousands of genes into a low-dimensional space we can visualize.

  2. PCs are directions of maximum variation: PC1 captures the strongest signal; PC2 captures the second strongest.

  3. Distance equals similarity: Samples that cluster close together on a PCA plot have similar expression profiles.

  4. Use PCA for quality control: Color by technical variables first to detect batch effects, and audit gene loadings for biological sanity.

Required Reading

  • Bioconductor PCA Workflow: Practice loading a count table in R, centering the data, running prcomp(), and plotting the results.

Johnson’s Rule: A PCA plot is the mirror of your experiment. If your samples cluster by the day they were prepped rather than the drug they were treated with, no statistical trick can save your analysis. Find the batch confounder before you write the manuscript.
