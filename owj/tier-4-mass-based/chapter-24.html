Chapter 24: Lipidomics and Other Mass Spectrometry Assays

Published

June 5, 2026

Modified

July 5, 2026

Johnson’s First Principle: Lipids Are Active Cellular Gatekeepers, Not Passive Fat Storage

I suggest you discard the textbook view of lipids as mere energy-storing fat droplets. Lipids are structural barriers, signaling mediators, and anchors for membrane-spanning proteins. They dictate membrane fluidity, organize microdomains (lipid rafts), and initiate signaling cascades that control cell survival and death. Because lipids are highly hydrophobic and structurally complex, they require specialized extraction and analytical protocols. If you want to understand membrane biophysics or lipid-mediated signaling, you must measure lipids using dedicated lipidomic workflows.

Core Concepts

What Is Being Measured and at What Molecular Level

  • What Is Being Measured: Hydrophobic small molecules (fatty acids, glycerolipids, glycerophospholipids, sphingolipids, saccharolipids, polyketides, sterols, prenols).

  • Molecular Level: The cellular membrane structure and lipid signaling mediator levels. Lipidomics characterizes the diversity of lipid species, tracking their specific fatty acid tail lengths and saturation states.

The Chemical Complexity of Lipids

I recommend you appreciate the structural complexity that makes lipid identification particularly challenging:

  • Isobaric Species: Many lipids share the exact same nominal mass but differ in their elemental composition (isobaric) or their structural configuration (isomeric). For example, PC(34:1) represents a phosphatidylcholine with 34 carbons and 1 double bond in its fatty acid tails, but this could be composed of PC(16:0/18:1) or PC(18:0/16:1).

  • Double-Bond Positions: The position (\(sn-1\) vs \(sn-2\)) and stereochemistry (cis vs trans) of double bonds alters the lipid’s physical properties but is difficult to resolve using conventional mass spectrometry.

Specialized Extraction Protocols

Unlike hydrophilic metabolites, lipids require organic solvents for separation from proteins and polar molecules. I will teach you the three major extraction methods:

  1. Folch Extraction: Uses a mixture of chloroform and methanol (2:1, v/v) with water. The lipids partition into the lower chloroform phase. It is the historical gold standard for total lipid recovery but chloroform is toxic and the lower phase is difficult to pipet without contamination.

  2. Bligh and Dyer Extraction: Similar to Folch but optimized for biological samples with high water content, adjusting the solvent ratio to achieve a monophasic mixture before adding water to split phases.

  3. MTBE (Methyl-tert-butyl ether) Extraction: Uses MTBE and methanol. The major advantage: lipids partition into the upper organic phase. This makes automated pipetting and high-throughput workflows much cleaner, as you can draw off the lipid layer without disturbing the protein pellet or the aqueous phase at the bottom.

Analytical Strategies: Shotgun vs. LC-MS

I recommend you understand the two competing paradigms for analyzing extracted lipids:

  • Shotgun Lipidomics (Direct Infusion): Lipids are infused directly into the mass spectrometer (often using nanoelectrospray) without chromatographic separation.

    • Advantages: Fast, highly reproducible, and enables constant spray conditions for quantitative comparisons.

    • Limitations: Susceptible to severe ion suppression, and cannot separate structural isomers.

  • LC-MS Lipidomics: Lipids are separated using reverse-phase (C18 or C8) or normal-phase (HILIC) liquid chromatography before entering the mass spectrometer.

    • Advantages: Reduces ion suppression, resolves many isomeric lipid species, and provides retention time as an orthogonal identification metric.

    • Limitations: Longer run times, and retention time drift requires alignment algorithms.

Resolving Isomers: Ion Mobility Spectrometry (IMS)

An emerging dimension in lipidomics is Ion Mobility Spectrometry. IMS separates ions in the gas phase based on their size, shape, and charge as they drift through a buffer gas under the influence of an electric field.

  • Collision Cross Section (CCS): The physical cross-sectional area of a lipid ion. CCS is a highly reproducible physical property that depends on the lipid’s three-dimensional shape. By combining \(m/z\), retention time, and CCS, you can confidently distinguish lipid isomers (such as determining the double-bond position) that are identical by mass spectrometry alone.

Biological Interpretation

The Reviewer Mindset: The Oxidation Warning

Lipids containing unsaturated fatty acids are highly susceptible to auto-oxidation when exposed to oxygen, light, or heat. This process (lipid peroxidation) creates artifactual oxidized lipid species and destroys the native lipids.

If you are reviewing a lipidomics study, I recommend you look for these quality control steps:

  • Antioxidants: Did the authors add antioxidants like BHT (butylated hydroxytoluene) during extraction?

  • Storage Conditions: Were the samples stored at \(-80^\circ\text{C}\) under an atmosphere of nitrogen or argon? Storing lipid extracts at \(-20^\circ\text{C}\) in standard air for months will lead to extensive oxidation, rendering the dataset invalid.

Dynamic Membrane Remodeling

The specific composition of lipids determines membrane biophysics. I suggest you focus on:

  • The Saturation Index: Cancer cells often remodel their membranes by increasing the proportion of saturated and monounsaturated fatty acids relative to polyunsaturated fatty acids. This makes the membrane more resistant to lipid peroxidation and ferroptosis (an iron-dependent form of non-apoptotic cell death), protecting the tumor from chemotherapy.

  • Signaling Cascades: Cleavage of membrane lipids by phospholipases releases signaling mediators (such as arachidonic acid, the precursor for pro-inflammatory eicosanoids). Understanding these dynamics requires tracking the free fatty acids alongside the parent membrane phospholipids.

Current Landscape

  • LIPID MAPS Consortium: The international classification system and reference database that standardizes lipid nomenclature, structural definitions, and mass spectra.

  • LipidSearch and MS-DIAL: High-throughput software tools designed specifically for peak detection, alignment, and annotation of lipid species from LC-MS/MS data.

  • Single-Cell Lipidomics: Emerging microfluidic platforms coupled with high-resolution MS are starting to profile lipid compositions at single-cell resolution, though sensitivity limits remain a major bottleneck.

Summary and Required Reading

  1. Lipids are dynamic gatekeepers: They dictate membrane biophysics and cell signaling, requiring distinct extraction and ionization protocols.

  2. Choose your extraction carefully: MTBE extraction is preferred for high-throughput pipelines because the lipids partition into the easily accessible upper phase.

  3. Shotgun vs. LC-MS: Shotgun is fast and reproducible; LC-MS resolves isomers and reduces ion suppression.

  4. Ion Mobility adds the CCS dimension: CCS measurements resolve structural isomers based on physical shape rather than mass alone.

  5. Unsaturated lipids oxidize rapidly: Protect samples with antioxidants (BHT) and inert gas storage to prevent technical artifacts.

Required Reading

  • Fahy et al.: “Update of the LIPID MAPS comprehensive classification system for lipids” (Journal of Lipid Research, 2009).

Johnson’s Rule: Lipids are the physical walls of the cell’s compartments. If you do not measure the wall composition, you cannot understand how the molecular machines inside are isolated or regulated.
