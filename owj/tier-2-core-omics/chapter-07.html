Chapter 7: Sequencing Physics, Chemistry, and Raw Formats

Published

June 5, 2026

Modified

July 5, 2026

You Cannot Process a File Correctly If You Do Not Understand the Physics of the Machine That Generated It

A sequencing file (such as a FASTQ file) is not just abstract text on your screen; it is a digital recording of a physical chemistry experiment. The numbers, characters, and coordinates inside these files represent light signals, electrical currents, and chemical reactions that occurred inside a sequencing instrument. If you treat these files as generic databases without understanding the hardware chemistry under the hood, your downstream analyses will be fundamentally blind to technical errors.

Core Concepts

A Brief History: From Capillaries to Massive Parallelization

For decades, the gold standard of sequencing was Sanger Sequencing. Developed by Frederick Sanger in 1977, this method relies on chain-termination chemistry.

  1. The Chemistry: DNA polymerase copies a target strand using standard nucleotides (dNTPs) mixed with a small concentration of fluorescently labeled chain-terminating nucleotides (ddNTPs).

  2. The Measurement: Whenever a ddNTP is incorporated, synthesis stops. This creates a mixture of DNA fragments of every possible length, each ending with a colored label. These fragments are separated by size using capillary electrophoresis, and a laser reads the color of each fragment as it passes by.

While Sanger sequencing is highly accurate and yields long reads (up to 900 base pairs), it has a catastrophic bottleneck: it can only sequence one DNA molecule (or one PCR product) per capillary run. It is targeted, slow, and expensive.

The Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS) revolution solved this bottleneck by replacing single-capillary runs with Massive Parallelization. Instead of reading one long fragment at a time, NGS instruments sequence billions of short DNA fragments simultaneously on a single glass surface (a flow cell). This dropped the cost of sequencing a million bases by more than 100,000-fold, transitioning genomics from a boutique academic luxury to a central pillar of clinical medicine.

Why NGS Dominates the Field: Clinical and Basic Applications

The high throughput of NGS makes it the default tool for both discovery research and medical diagnostics:

  • Pathogen Tracking (Genomic Epidemiology): During the COVID-19 pandemic, researchers worldwide used NGS to sequence SARS-CoV-2 genomes directly from patient samples. By comparing mutational changes across thousands of viral genomes, systems like Nextstrain mapped the evolution and spread of variants (like Omicron or Delta) in real time.

  • Non-Invasive Prenatal Testing (NIPT): Clinicians sequence cell-free DNA (cfDNA) circulating in the mother’s blood. By counting the relative proportion of reads mapping to Chromosome 21 versus other chromosomes, software can identify fetal trisomies (Down syndrome) without invasive amniocentesis.

  • Oncology Liquid Biopsies: Sequencing circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) in a patient’s blood to detect rare somatic mutations, enabling early cancer detection and real-time treatment monitoring.

Illumina Chemistry: Sequencing-by-Synthesis Under the Hood

The dominant technology in the NGS market is Illumina’s Sequencing-by-Synthesis (SBS).

To understand what you are measuring, you must understand the four steps of the Illumina protocol:

  1. Library Preparation: DNA is sheared into short fragments (~200–500 bp). Synthetic adapter sequences are ligated (attached) to both ends of each fragment. These adapters contain barcodes (indices) that allow you to mix multiple samples together in the same run, along with sequencing primer binding sites.

  2. Cluster Generation (Bridge Amplification): The DNA library is loaded onto a glass flow cell covered in short oligonucleotide lawns. The adapters bind to these oligos. Through a series of isothermal PCR cycles, each single DNA molecule is amplified into a dense physical cluster of ~1,000 identical clonal copies. This clustering is necessary because a single molecule does not emit enough light to be detected by the camera.

  3. Reversible Terminator Incorporation: The instrument floods the flow cell with DNA polymerase and four modified nucleotides. Each nucleotide has a unique fluorescent dye (red, green, blue, or yellow) and a reversible blocking group that prevents the polymerase from adding more than one base at a time.

  4. Imaging and Cleavage: The unbound nucleotides are washed away. A laser excites the fluorescent dyes, and a high-resolution camera takes a picture of the flow cell. The color of the light emitted by each cluster tells the instrument which base was added. Finally, the blocking group and dye are chemically cleaved (removed), allowing the next nucleotide to bind in the next cycle.

Alternatives and Technology Evolution: Long-Read sequencing

While Illumina’s short-read chemistry is highly accurate and cheap, short reads (~150 bp) cannot easily span large structural variants, repeat regions, or highly homologous genes. This bottleneck drove the evolution of long-read sequencing technologies:

  • Pacific Biosciences (PacBio) SMRT Sequencing: This chemistry uses Zero-Mode Waveguides (ZMWs)—billions of tiny metallic wells where a single DNA polymerase is anchored at the bottom. As fluorescently labeled bases are incorporated in real time, the camera records the light pulse. By attaching circular adapters to the DNA (Circular Consensus Sequencing or CCS), the polymerase circles the template multiple times, allowing the random errors to be averaged out, yielding highly accurate (Q30) long reads (>10 kb).

  • Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT) Sequencing: Instead of using light, ONT measures ionic current. A motor protein feeds a single strand of DNA through a protein pore embedded in an electrically charged membrane. Each combination of bases (k-mers) blocking the pore produces a distinct drop in the electrical current. A neural network basecaller decodes these electrical fluctuations directly into sequence strings. ONT can sequence molecules of arbitrary length (up to megabases), but it is prone to systematic errors in homopolymer regions (e.g., distinguishing a run of 6 Adenines from 7 Adenines).

From Raw Camera Signals to FASTQ

The direct output of an Illumina sequencer is a directory of binary base call files called BCL files. BCL files record the fluorescent color detected at each cluster coordinate for every cycle of the run.

To convert these raw coordinate signals into a usable format, you must run a tool like bcl2fastq or bcl-convert. This tool performs two critical steps:

  1. Base Calling: Converts the color signals into DNA letters (A, C, G, T) and assigns a quality score to each call.

  2. Demultiplexing: Reads the short index barcode sequences on the adapters and assigns each read to its corresponding sample file.

The output of this process is a set of raw FASTQ files.

Decoding the FASTQ Specification

A FASTQ file contains four lines per read:

@SRR1234567.1 1/1
GATCGATCGATCGATCGATCGATCGATCGATCGATCGATC
+
F:FFA9FEE,EE.E/FFEF<F:FF/FF:F8FFF9FFDFD/

  • Line 1 (The Header): Always starts with @ and contains the read name, sequencing instrument ID, flow cell coordinates, and sample index/lane details.

  • Line 2 (The Sequence): The raw base calls (A, C, G, T, or N for undetermined bases).

  • Line 3 (The Spacer): Always starts with + and is sometimes followed by a repetition of the header.

  • Line 4 (The Quality Scores): A string of ASCII characters matching the length of the sequence on Line 2. Each character represents a Phred quality score.

Understanding Quality Scores (The Phred Scale)

The quality string encodes the probability (\(P_e\)) that a base call is incorrect. The Phred score (\(Q\)) is calculated using a logarithmic scale: \[Q = -10 \log_{10}(P_e)\]

A higher Phred score means a lower error probability. To represent these values as a single character in text files, the Phred score is offset by 33 (or 64 in older pipelines) and mapped to the standard ASCII character table (Phred+33).

For example, a Phred score of 30 (\(Q = 30\)) has an ASCII value of \(30 + 33 = 63\), which corresponds to the character ?.

Character Phred Score (\(Q\)) Error Probability (\(P_e\)) Accuracy Meaning in Practice
* 9 12.5% 87.5% Highly unreliable
5 20 1.0% 99.0% Standard minimum
? 30 0.1% 99.9% High-quality call
I 40 0.01% 99.99% Excellent accuracy

Clues for Quantitative Analysis: The Relative Nature of Counts

Before we align these reads, you must internalize two biological and mathematical realities of sequencing data. We will rely heavily on these clues in later chapters:

  1. Reads are Discrete Count Data: A FASTQ file contains individual, integer counts of reads. You cannot observe a fraction of a read. Therefore, continuous normal statistics are fundamentally inappropriate for raw sequence data (Chapter 4).

  2. Sequencing yields Relative (Compositional) Quantifications: The sequencer has a fixed capacity (e.g., 300 million reads per lane). If Sample A has twice as many reads as Sample B, it does not mean Sample A had a higher starting concentration of molecules in the tube. It only means Sample A took up a larger fraction of the sequencer’s total output. Read counts are relative measurements, and they must be normalized for sequencing depth and library composition before any biological comparison can be made.

WGS vs. WES: The Physical Mechanics of Target Enrichment

Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS) and Whole Exome Sequencing (WES) are not just different computational analysis pathways; they represent entirely distinct physical library preparations with different biochemical constraints.

  • The Physical Bias of Capture: WGS sequences the entire genome, typically using a PCR-free library preparation that minimizes GC-bias and PCR amplification artifacts. WES, by contrast, relies on a hybridization capture step. After DNA fragmentation, biotinylated oligonucleotide probes (baits) designed to target the exonic regions (representing ~1.5% of the human genome) are hybridized to the library. These probe-bound fragments are then physically pulled down using streptavidin-coated magnetic beads.

  • Targeted Efficiency vs. Genomic Fairness: WES is an “efficiency-first” approach based on the empirical observation that ~85% of known disease-causing mutations reside in coding exons. However, this hybrid-capture process introduces severe physical limitations:

    1. GC Bias and Capture Efficiency: Hybridization is highly sensitive to melting temperature (\(T_m\)), resulting in poor capture efficiency in extremely GC-rich or AT-rich exons, causing “cold spots” where sequencing coverage is zero despite the region being coding.
    2. Regulatory and Intronic Blind Spots: WES is completely blind to deep intronic mutations that cause aberrant splicing, and to regulatory mutations in enhancers, promoters, and non-coding RNAs.
    3. Evenness of Coverage: WGS provides “fair” and relatively even coverage across the genome. WES coverage is highly uneven, with peak-and-valley distributions driven by probe binding affinities. Thus, while WES is cost-effective for identifying coding mutations, WGS is far superior for structural variant detection, copy number analysis, and identifying novel non-coding regulatory variants.

Biological Interpretation

When auditing a raw dataset, you must look beyond the raw sequence and examine the quality score patterns.

Positional Quality Decay (The 3’ Drop)

If you plot the average Phred scores across the length of Illumina reads, you will notice a systematic decay: quality scores are highest at the 5’ beginning of the read and drop at the 3’ end.

This drop is driven by the physical chemistry of the flow cell. Over 150 cycles of synthesis, two technical issues accumulate:

  • Phasing: A small percentage of clonal copy molecules fail to incorporate a base during a cycle, falling behind.

  • Pre-phasing: Some molecules incorporate two bases, skipping ahead.

As the molecules in a cluster lose synchrony, the fluorescent signal becomes mixed (blurry). The instrument can no longer confidently distinguish which base is being added, and the Phred score drops. If your quality scores drop below Q20 at the 3’ end, you may need to trim the reads before alignment to prevent false-positive mapping.

Summary and Required Reading

  1. Sanger is targeted; NGS is parallel: Massive parallelization dropped sequencing costs and made genome-wide assays clinical realities.

  2. Illumina sequences by synthesis: Reversible terminators incorporate one fluorescent base per cycle; coordinate images are translated from BCL files to FASTQ.

  3. Phred scores are logarithmic: \(Q = -10 \log_{10}(P_e)\). A Q30 score represents a 99.9% base call accuracy.

  4. Counts are relative: Read counts reflect the proportion of a molecule in the sequencing library, not its absolute biological concentration.

Required Reading

  • Cock et al.: “The Sanger FASTQ file format for sequences with quality scores” (Nucleic Acids Research, 2010). A historical and technical breakdown of Phred encoding.

Johnson’s Rule: A FASTQ file is the raw signal of a physical chemistry reaction. If your quality score plots show sudden drops in the middle of a run, the chemistry failed on the flow cell. Never trust an aligner to correct for a failing instrument.
